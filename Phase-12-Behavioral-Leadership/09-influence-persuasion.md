## Why This Exists

In engineering, you often need others to:
- Adopt your technical approach
- Prioritize your project
- Change their process
- Invest in your idea
- Support your decision

You rarely have formal authority to force these outcomes. You need influence.

FAANG companies ask about influence because they need engineers who can:
1. **Drive change without authority**: Make things happen through persuasion, not position
2. **Build coalitions**: Get multiple people/teams aligned
3. **Overcome resistance**: Handle skepticism and pushback
4. **Create momentum**: Turn ideas into action

The best engineers don't just build things. They convince others to build the right things.

---

## What Breaks Without Influence Skills

Without the ability to influence:

- **Good ideas die**: No one adopts better approaches
- **Technical debt accumulates**: No one champions improvements
- **Bad decisions persist**: No one challenges the status quo
- **Innovation stalls**: New ideas never get traction
- **You become isolated**: People stop listening to your suggestions

**The interview signal**: An engineer who cannot demonstrate influence may be a passive contributor who waits for direction rather than creating it.

## The Influence Framework

### Step 1: Understand Your Audience

Before you can influence, you must understand who you're trying to influence and why they might resist.

**Questions to answer:**
- What are their goals and priorities?
- What are their concerns or constraints?
- What do they value? (Speed, quality, simplicity, innovation?)
- What would success look like for them?
- What would make them look good?

**Example:**
> "I wanted to convince the team to adopt a new testing framework. Before proposing it, I talked to the QA lead. I learned they were concerned about learning curve and maintenance burden. They valued reliability over new features. So I framed the proposal around reducing flaky tests and making maintenance easier, not just 'using new tech.'"

### Step 2: Build Your Case with Data

Opinions are easy to dismiss. Data is harder to argue with.

**Types of data to gather:**
- Performance metrics (before/after comparisons)
- Cost analysis (time, money, resources)
- Risk assessment (what happens if we don't change?)
- Success stories (how others have benefited)
- Proof of concepts (demonstrate feasibility)

**Example:**
> "Instead of saying 'we should use Redis for caching,' I ran a 2-week experiment. I showed that Redis reduced API latency by 60% and cut database load by 40%. I also calculated that the infrastructure cost was $200/month, but the reduced database load saved $500/month. The data made the decision obvious."

### Step 3: Address Concerns Proactively

Don't wait for objections. Anticipate them and address them in your proposal.

**Common concerns:**
- "This will take too long to implement"
- "We don't have expertise in this"
- "This adds complexity"
- "What if it doesn't work?"
- "We've tried this before"

**How to address:**
- Acknowledge the concern: "I understand you're worried about..."
- Provide data: "Here's why I think it's manageable..."
- Offer mitigation: "We can reduce risk by..."
- Show precedent: "Here's how [similar team] handled this..."

### Step 4: Start Small, Build Momentum

Big changes are scary. Small wins build confidence.

**Approach:**
1. Propose a pilot or experiment
2. Set success criteria upfront
3. Execute and measure
4. Share results
5. Expand based on success

**Example:**
> "I wanted to convince the organization to adopt automated code reviews. Instead of proposing a company-wide rollout, I proposed a 1-month pilot with my team. We measured time saved, code quality improvements, and developer satisfaction. After the pilot, I presented results showing 30% faster reviews and 40% fewer bugs. Three other teams asked to join. Within 6 months, it was standard practice."

### Step 5: Find Champions

One person can't influence an organization alone. Build a coalition.

**Who to recruit:**
- Early adopters who are excited about change
- Influential people whose support matters
- Skeptics who, once convinced, become powerful advocates
- People who benefit from the change

**Example:**
> "I wanted to convince leadership to invest in better monitoring. I first got buy-in from the on-call engineers who were tired of being woken up by false alarms. They became champions. Then I got support from the engineering manager who saw the productivity impact. Together, we presented to leadership with data and a clear ROI. The proposal was approved."

### Step 6: Make It Easy to Say Yes

Remove friction from adoption.

**Ways to reduce friction:**
- Provide templates or examples
- Offer to do the initial setup
- Create documentation
- Provide training
- Offer ongoing support

**Example:**
> "I wanted teams to adopt a new deployment process. Instead of just documenting it, I created a migration script, wrote step-by-step guides, and offered to pair with each team for their first deployment. I made it so easy that teams were asking to migrate."

## Types of Influence Stories

### Type 1: Technical Adoption

Convincing others to use a technology, tool, or approach.

**What to show:**
- Why the current approach was insufficient
- How you evaluated alternatives
- How you built the case with data
- How you handled resistance
- The outcome and adoption

### Type 2: Process Change

Convincing others to change how they work.

**What to show:**
- The inefficiency you identified
- The process you proposed
- How you got buy-in
- How you implemented
- The improvement achieved

### Type 3: Priority Influence

Convincing others to prioritize your work or idea.

**What to show:**
- Why your work should be prioritized
- How you made the business case
- How you navigated competing priorities
- The impact of prioritizing your work

### Type 4: Strategic Direction

Influencing organizational or technical strategy.

**What to show:**
- The strategic problem you identified
- Your proposed direction
- How you built consensus
- How you influenced leadership
- The strategic impact

## Example: Complete Influence Story

### Question: "Tell me about a time you convinced others to adopt your idea."

---

### Situation (20 seconds)

> "I was a senior engineer on a team that was struggling with deployment reliability. We had 3-4 production incidents per month, mostly from configuration errors or dependency mismatches. The team was frustrated, and leadership was concerned about reliability. I had an idea to adopt infrastructure-as-code using Terraform, but the team was resistant because they thought it would slow down deployments."

**What this establishes:**
- Problem (deployment reliability)
- Scale (3-4 incidents/month)
- Your idea (Terraform)
- Resistance (team thought it would slow things down)

---

### Task (15 seconds)

> "I needed to convince the team that infrastructure-as-code would actually improve our reliability and speed, not slow us down. I also needed to get buy-in from my manager who was concerned about the learning curve and time investment."

**What this establishes:**
- Multiple stakeholders (team, manager)
- The challenge (overcoming resistance)
- Your goal (adoption of infrastructure-as-code)

---

### Action (2 minutes)

> "First, I needed to understand the resistance. I talked to team members individually. I learned they were worried about: the learning curve, slower initial deployments, and maintenance overhead. They also didn't see how it would help with the current problems.
>
> I addressed each concern with data and examples:
>
> For the learning curve, I found that other teams in the company had adopted Terraform. I talked to them and learned the basics took about 2 days. I proposed we'd do a 1-day workshop together.
>
> For deployment speed, I built a proof-of-concept. I took one of our services and converted it to Terraform. I showed that while the first deployment took 5 minutes longer (to validate the config), subsequent deployments were actually 2 minutes faster because we eliminated manual steps. I also showed that rollbacks were instant instead of taking 10 minutes.
>
> For the reliability concern, I analyzed our last 6 months of incidents. I found that 60% were caused by configuration drift or manual errors. I showed how infrastructure-as-code would prevent these by making configurations version-controlled and repeatable.
>
> I created a one-page proposal with these findings and presented it to the team. I acknowledged their concerns upfront and showed how I'd addressed each one. I also proposed a pilot: we'd convert one non-critical service first, measure the impact, and then decide whether to expand.
>
> One senior engineer was still skeptical. He said we'd tried similar things before and they didn't work. I listened to his concerns and learned that a previous attempt failed because it was too complex. I proposed a simpler approach: we'd start with just our staging environment, not production. This reduced risk and made it easier to try.
>
> I got approval for the pilot. I led the conversion of our staging environment, documented the process, and trained the team. After 2 weeks, we had zero configuration-related incidents in staging, compared to 2 in the previous 2 weeks. Deployment time was actually faster.
>
> I presented these results to the team and my manager. The skeptical engineer became a champion, saying he was wrong to resist. We expanded to production, and within 3 months, all services were using infrastructure-as-code."

**What this demonstrates:**
- Understanding resistance (individual conversations)
- Data-driven approach (POC, incident analysis)
- Addressing concerns proactively (learning curve, speed, reliability)
- Compromise (pilot, staging first)
- Converting skeptics (senior engineer became champion)
- Measurable results (zero incidents, faster deployments)

---

### Result (25 seconds)

> "Within 6 months, we reduced production incidents from 3-4 per month to less than 1 per month. Deployment time improved by 20%, and rollbacks became instant instead of taking 10 minutes. The team was happier because they spent less time fighting fires.
>
> My manager asked me to present this approach to other teams. Three other teams adopted it, and it became a company-wide best practice.
>
> What I learned is that influence requires understanding resistance, not just presenting benefits. When I addressed people's specific concerns with data, they became advocates instead of blockers."

**What this demonstrates:**
- Quantified impact (3-4 to <1 incidents, 20% faster deployments)
- Organizational impact (other teams adopted, company-wide practice)
- Personal growth (learning about understanding resistance)

## Influence Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Bulldozer

**Behavior**: Pushing your idea aggressively, dismissing others' concerns.

**Problem**: Creates resistance, damages relationships, ideas get rejected.

**Better approach**: Listen to concerns, address them, build consensus.

### Anti-Pattern 2: The Perfectionist

**Behavior**: Waiting until your proposal is perfect before sharing.

**Problem**: Misses opportunities, others solve the problem first.

**Better approach**: Share early, iterate based on feedback.

### Anti-Pattern 3: The Lone Wolf

**Behavior**: Trying to influence alone without building a coalition.

**Problem**: Limited reach, easy to dismiss, burns out.

**Better approach**: Find champions, build a coalition.

### Anti-Pattern 4: The Assumer

**Behavior**: Assuming others see the problem the same way you do.

**Problem**: Misalignment, resistance you didn't expect.

**Better approach**: Understand others' perspectives first.

### Anti-Pattern 5: The Quitter

**Behavior**: Giving up after first rejection.

**Problem**: Good ideas die, you get a reputation for not following through.

**Better approach**: Understand the rejection, address concerns, try again.

## Writing Technical Proposals (RFC/ADR)

### What is an RFC/ADR?

**RFC (Request for Comments)**: A proposal for a significant technical change that needs review and approval.

**ADR (Architecture Decision Record)**: A document that records an important architectural decision and its context.

### Structure of a Strong Proposal

**1. Problem Statement**
- What problem are we solving?
- Why does it matter now?
- What happens if we don't solve it?

**2. Goals and Non-Goals**
- What are we trying to achieve?
- What are we explicitly NOT trying to achieve?

**3. Proposed Solution**
- What is your recommendation?
- How does it work at a high level?
- What are the key components?

**4. Alternatives Considered**
- What other options did you evaluate?
- Why did you reject them?
- What are the trade-offs?

**5. Implementation Plan**
- How would we implement this?
- What's the timeline?
- What are the milestones?
- What resources are needed?

**6. Risks and Mitigation**
- What could go wrong?
- How likely is each risk?
- How would we mitigate them?

**7. Success Criteria**
- How will we know if this worked?
- What metrics will we track?
- What's the definition of success?

**8. Open Questions**
- What still needs to be decided?
- What input do you need from others?

### Tips for Writing Proposals

1. **Start with the problem**: Help readers understand why this matters
2. **Be concise**: Long proposals don't get read
3. **Use data**: Numbers are more convincing than opinions
4. **Address concerns proactively**: Don't wait for objections
5. **Make it easy to review**: Use clear structure, diagrams, examples
6. **Ask for specific feedback**: "Does this approach make sense?" not "Thoughts?"

## Influence at Different Levels

### Junior/Mid-Level (L3-L4)

Influence scope:
- Proposing improvements to your team
- Suggesting tools or approaches for your work
- Influencing feature implementation details

What interviewers look for:
- You can make a case for your ideas
- You seek input and incorporate feedback
- You can get buy-in from peers

### Senior (L5)

Influence scope:
- Proposing technical direction for projects
- Influencing team processes
- Getting buy-in across teams
- Writing RFCs for significant changes

What interviewers look for:
- You drive technical decisions
- You build consensus around your ideas
- You influence without authority
- You create proposals that get adopted

### Staff+ (L6+)

Influence scope:
- Influencing organizational technical strategy
- Setting standards and best practices
- Driving large-scale change initiatives
- Influencing executive decisions

What interviewers look for:
- You influence at organizational scale
- You build coalitions for change
- You create frameworks others use
- You're a trusted advisor to leadership

## Preparing Your Influence Stories

### Story Selection Criteria

Choose stories where:
- You had an idea others didn't initially support
- You built a case and got buy-in
- You overcame resistance
- The outcome was positive
- You can explain your approach

```

- [Data point 1]
- [Data point 2]
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Understand before persuading**: Know your audience's goals and concerns
2. **Data beats opinions**: Build your case with metrics and evidence
3. **Address concerns proactively**: Don't wait for objections
4. **Start small**: Pilots and experiments build momentum
5. **Build coalitions**: One person can't influence alone
6. **Make it easy**: Remove friction from adoption
7. **Convert skeptics**: They become your strongest advocates
8. **Write strong proposals**: Structure, data, and clarity matter
